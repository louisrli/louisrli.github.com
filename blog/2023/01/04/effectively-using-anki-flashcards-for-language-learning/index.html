Effectively Using Anki Flashcards for Language Learning

Published on 4 January 2023

I’ve been using Anki to study foreign languages for three years now, and I consistently spend about an hour a day using Anki to learn something. In this time, I’ve studied three languages to varying degrees with the help of Anki: Turkish, Russian, and Italian.

But truthfully, I can’t say that all that time spent doing Anki has always been effective. In fact, when I look back, I feel that I wasted a lot of time using Anki.

The goal of this article is to provide tips to use Anki more effectively for those who have already tried or are currently using it for language learning but may be using it ineffectively. It assumes background knowledge about how Anki works without explaining every detail or term for those who may not be familiar with Anki.

Tips for Effectively Using Anki

Study target language to native language

Suppose my native language is English, and I’m studying Russian. This means that I recommend learning all your cards with the English side on the front, and the Russian side on the back.

Some people like to learn the “recognition side” (Russian front, English back) first. I did this for over three thousand words in Russian, and in retrospect, I regretted it. I would often run into those same words in other contexts and simply not recognize them. In theory, learning this way should allow you to recognize many words. In practice, this doesn’t happen.

There are two problems with this approach. The first is that our brain has all types of ways to cheat remembering the definitions of those cards: “Oh yeah, there’s a card in this deck that means ‘to believe’, and it sounds/looks vaguely like this word.” The second is that being able to recognize a word in a foreign language is very, very far from being able to produce the syllables required to say it.

Having English in the front and Russian on the back forces you to produce the word so that you might eventually be able to use it in a conversation. You should also mark the word wrong even if you get one syllable or letter off, which will happen very frequently. We often remember that a word has certain letters or starts in a cetrain way, but we can’t always remember the full word.

It may seem strict to punish what seems like such a small mistake in recalling a word, but I’ve found that even a one letter mistake quite unforgiving. For example, if I said the word “onforgiving” instead of “unforgiving” to you, it could definitely be a source of confusion. Or “confasion” instead of “confusion.” Or “instod” instead of “instead.” You get the point. I can promise you that I’ve done this in real life with real words, and people did not understand what I meant to say.

Don’t spend too much time making your own cards.

Conventional advice states that, supposedly, the most “effective” learning comes from crafting your own cards. You’ll see comments like this everywhere about language learning cards. I disagree.

I want to emphasize that in this article, effectively means not only effectively in terms of learning but also in terms of time. It depends on one’s goals when learning a language, but in my opinion, to start comfortably having conversations about various topics, one really needs to know thousands of words.

First, part of being an effective learner is acknowledging the limits of your discipline. Creating your own cards is a painstaking process, and I don’t think that most people have the willpower to do this for thousands of words. If you do, more power to you. I’m talking here not about making cards for fifty something words, but for, let’s say, the 800 new words or phrases that are highlighted in the Kindle (this is about how many highlighted words I unload from my Kindle every time).

But in my perspective, making your own cards is simply not the most effective use of the limited time we have to learn a language. Ultimately, that’s why I wrote a script to quickly create high quality cards on scale.

Make cards for phrases and include prepositions

Even though we’ve been brainwashed to think of things in terms of words, the truth is that learning words in isolation can cause all types of problems for language learners.

When I was learning Turkish and partway through learning Russian, this was one of the biggest mistakes that I made. I would learn a word, but often I would use it incorrectly because words are not used in isolation, but instead, they’re often used with mandatory auxiliary parts.

For example, take the English word “believe.” You can say “I believe him”, or “I believe in him”, both of which have completely different meanings. In this case, if you were learning English, you might want one card for “believe X” and another card for “believe in X”.

It doesn’t always have to be about prepositions though. In general, I tend now to make cards for phrases instead of individual words. When I highlight words in my Kindle while reading, often I highlight phrases where I know all of the words contained inside of the phrase, but I never would have produced or thought of that combination myself.

Don’t be scared to edit cards to include prepositions. If I see a card with an isolated word but I see that the example sentences frequently or always occur with a certain preposition, I’ll often edit the card so that it tests the usage of the preposition too.

The above section is slightly abstract, since it’s hard to generalize to every language. But the point is – think about what you would need to learn to utilize in a sentence, and learn that unit, instead of the single unit of a word.

Make cards for things you understand but wouldn’t say

This is related to making cards for phrases. As I mentioned, when I read, I often highlight phrases (to make Anki cards later) that I completely understand. I know every word in the phrase. If I read it in a book, I would understand it instantly. If I heard it, I would understand it.

But after some time, I realized that this doesn’t mean that I could say it in a conversation.

The essence of this tip is the following: when you hear or read something in any context, think not only about whether you understood it, but whether you could or want to say it yourself.

I’ve made cards for very, very simple phrases – phrases that seem like they would be learned in the first two months of studying a language, but ones that I realized I had a gap in my knowledge. For every person, these will be different. In my case, I often find that I have a big gap in saying concrete, real life things, even though I understand them. When I read, I’m often marking simple phrases, like “he picked up the X” or “he went around the corner.”

At some point, this habit has extended even to my native language. When I hear or read something in English, I frequently consider whether or not I would ever say something in that way as well.

Add audio when possible

I’m (now) a huge advocate of adding audio to every card. It was quite common before for me to be able to read a word but mispronounce it if I spoke it or not recognize it if I heard it. This should rarely happen if a word is properly learned, and having audio on your card prevents that.

For audio, it should be possible on every card using an add-on like AwesomeTTS. I refrained from using AwesomeTTS from an embarrasingly long time, maybe until six months ago, because I was too lazy to figure out how to use it. It turns out it’s not that difficult.

Warning: AwesomeTTS might not always get the pronunciation of a word right either in some languages, so double check when possible. For example, on more obscure Russian words, it’ll often place the stress in the wrong place.
